The Science of Affirmations

By Ashwin Badhiye

For many, the idea of repeating positive statements to oneself in a mirror feels a bit cliché, perhaps even a little silly. However, the practice of affirmations is rooted in far more than just "positive thinking." There is a substantial body of psychological and neuroscientific research that explains exactly why and how affirmations work to reshape our reality and aid in manifestation.

Neuroplasticity: Rewiring the Brain

To understand affirmations, we must first understand neuroplasticity—the brain's remarkable ability to reorganize itself by forming new neural connections throughout life. Every time we think a thought, a specific neural pathway is activated. The more frequently that pathway is used, the stronger and more efficient it becomes. This is how habits are formed, both behavioral and mental.

If your default mental state leans toward self-doubt or scarcity (e.g., "I'll never get that promotion," or "I'm not good enough"), those specific neural pathways are deeply entrenched. Affirmations serve as an intentional disruption of these negative patterns. By consciously choosing to repeat statements of capability and abundance, you are quite literally building and strengthening new, positive neural pathways.

The Self-Affirmation Theory

Psychologist Claude Steele popularized the "Self-Affirmation Theory" in the late 1980s. The theory posits that humans are fundamentally motivated to maintain a globally positive self-image. When this image is threatened by stress, failure, or negative feedback, our defense mechanisms kick in, often leading to anxiety or self-sabotage.

Affirmations help restore this sense of self-competence. Research shows that engaging in self-affirmation activities—like writing down your core values or repeating positive truths—can decrease stress, increase well-being, and improve performance under pressure. By affirming your worth and capabilities, you widen your perspective, making you more resilient and open to opportunities that align with your goals.

The Role of the Reticular Activating System (RAS)

The Reticular Activating System (RAS) is a bundle of nerves at our brainstem that filters out unnecessary information so the important stuff gets through. Your RAS is the reason you can hear your name spoken across a noisy room, or why, after you buy a red car, you suddenly see red cars everywhere.

Affirmations act as instructions for your RAS. When you consistently affirm a goal or a state of being (e.g., "I am attracting abundance into my life"), you are programming your RAS to notice opportunities, people, and resources that align with that statement. It’s not magic; it’s selective attention. You are training your brain to see the pathways to your success.

Making Affirmations Effective

Not all affirmations are created equal. To maximize their psychological impact, keep these principles in mind:

  • Keep it in the present tense: Say "I am confident" rather than "I will be confident." This signals to your brain that the state is already true.
  • Attach emotion: An affirmation without feeling is just words. Try to genuinely feel the joy, peace, or excitement associated with the statement as you say or write it.
  • Make it believable: If "I am a millionaire" feels like a lie, your brain will reject it. Try a bridging affirmation like, "I am increasingly open to new avenues of wealth."
